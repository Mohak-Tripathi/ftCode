WELL Building Standard Benchmark

TVOC Level (ug/m3) TVOC Index Value Level of Concern Symptoms/Effects (8 hours Exposure) Action Recommendation
Less than 300 0 Low No irritation or discomfort is expected. 1. Continue regular office operations and maintain good air quality practices.
2. Regularly inspect and maintain HVAC systems to ensure optimal function.
300 to 500 1 Acceptable Occasional irritation or discomfort may be possible with sensitive individuals. 1. Monitor sources of VOCs such as office equipment, cleaning agents, and building materials.
2. Ensure sufficient ventilation, especially in areas with potential VOC sources.
500 to 1000 2 Marginal Complaints about irritation and discomfort are possible in sensitive individuals. 1. Increase ventilation in the affected areas.
2. Identify and address specific VOC sources.
3. Consider using air purifiers equipped with activated carbon filters to reduce VOC levels.
1000 to 3000 3 High Irritation and discomfort may begin in general people. 1. Prioritize identifying and removing the sources of VOCs.
2. Temporarily restrict access to areas with high TVOC levels until the situation is resolved.
3. Increase air exchange rates and use air purifiers to rapidly reduce concentrations.
More than 3000 4 Very High Irritation and discomfort begins in general people. 1. Immediately evacuate and restrict access to areas with extremely high TVOC levels.
2. Engage environmental health experts to address the issue and provide specific interventions.
3. Thoroughly ventilate and possibly decontaminate the area before allowing reoccupation.
CO2 Lvl (PPM) CO2 Indx Vl Level of Concern Symptoms/Effects (8 hours Exposure) Action Recommendation
250-400 0 Low Normal background concentration in outdoor ambient air 1. Maintain regular operations and HVAC system checks.
2. Ensure HVAC systems are operating optimally.
400-1,000 1 Acceptable Concentrations typical of occupied indoor spaces with good air exchange. 1. Regularly check the ventilation system for proper air exchange.
2. Educate staff about the importance of taking breaks and stepping outside occasionally.
1,000-2,000 2 Marginal Complaints of drowsiness and poor air 1. Increase ventilation rates in the building.
2. Identify and address possible sources or areas of poor ventilation.
3. Use plants known for improving indoor air quality.
2,000-5,000 3 High Headaches, sleepiness and stagnant, stale, stuffy air. Poor concentration, loss of attention, increased heart rate and slight nausea 1. Urgently address and improve ventilation systems.
2. Consider using CO2 scrubbers or similar technologies if applicable.
3. Encourage regular breaks for employees to get fresh air.
>5,000 4 Very High Exposure may lead to serious oxygen deprivation resulting in permanent brain damage, coma and even death. 1. Evacuate the area immediately.
2. Consult a specialist to identify the source of the extreme CO2 concentration and address it urgently.
3. Reoccupy the space only after thorough inspection and remediation.
PM2.5 Level (ug/m3) PM Index Value Level of Concern Symptoms/Effects (8 hours Exposure) Action Recommendation
0-15 0 Low Satisfactory air quality posing little or no acute risk 1. Regularly maintain HVAC and air filtration systems.
2. Avoid introduction of potential pollutants.
15-25 1 Acceptable Moderate concerns in unusually susceptible individuals 1. Check and replace HVAC filters more frequently.
2. Limit activities that generate PM (like certain office equipment or maintenance tasks).
25-35 2 Marginal Unhealthy for susceptible groups (older adults with chronic heart or lung disease, children and asthmatics) 1. Install air purifiers in areas with high PM concentrations.
2. Review and optimize building ventilation.
3. Use entryway mats to reduce particulates from footwear.
35-50 3 High Unhealthy, General public may begin to experience health effects 1. Restrict outdoor air intake if outdoor PM levels are high.
2. Seal off areas undergoing renovations or other activities producing PM.
3. Regularly clean and vacuum using devices with HEPA filters.
>50 4 Very High Unhealthy, General public begin to experience health effects 1. Consider temporarily shutting down the office or allowing remote work until levels decrease.
2. Investigate sources of high PM levels and address them directly.
3. Consult with an indoor air quality expert for more tailored solutions.
PM1.0 Level (µg/m³) PM Index Value Level of Concern Symptoms/Effects (8 hours Exposure) Action Recommendation
0-5 0 Low Satisfactory air quality with minimal risk. 1. Maintain regular air quality monitoring.
2. Implement general air quality management practices.
5-10 1 Acceptable Low concerns, particularly for sensitive individuals. 1. Continue regular air quality monitoring.
2. Consider targeted actions in areas with higher concentrations, such as improving ventilation or using air purifiers.
10-20 2 Marginal Unhealthy for sensitive groups; general public not likely to be affected. 1. Increase air quality monitoring frequency.
2. Identify and address specific sources of PM1, such as combustion processes or indoor pollutants.
3. Consider implementing ventilation improvements and air purification systems.
20-35 3 High Unhealthy; general public may begin to experience health effects. 1. Implement immediate actions to reduce PM1 concentrations, including targeted source control.
2. Enhance ventilation systems and consider the use of high-efficiency air purifiers.
3. Communicate health advisories and encourage sensitive individuals to take precautions.
>35 4 Very High Health alert; everyone may begin to experience more serious health effects. 1. Implement emergency measures to reduce exposure, such as relocating individuals from affected areas.
2. Engage with local health authorities and consider temporary shutdowns or restrictions until air quality is improved.
3. Investigate and address sources of elevated PM1 levels and implement long-term air quality management strategies.

Temperature Level (⁰C) Comfort Index Value Level of Concern Symptoms/Effects (8 hours Exposure) Action Recommendation
<18 2 Marginal Too cold for most employees. Possible decrease in dexterity and increase in discomfort. 1. Check the HVAC system for potential issues such as malfunctioning thermostats, blocked vents, or inefficient heating elements.
2. Increase heating to bring the temperature into a more acceptable range. If HVAC isn't effective, it may need repair or calibration.
3. If certain areas are colder than others, inspect the HVAC system's distribution and airflow for imbalances.
4. Check for drafts, poorly insulated windows, and doors that might be affecting the HVAC's ability to maintain temperatures.
18-20 0 Low Might be slightly cool for some employees. 1. Ensure heating systems are functional in colder seasons.
20-24 1 Acceptable Ideal temperature for most office tasks and generally comfortable for most people. 1. Regularly monitor and maintain this temperature range.
2. Address any localized issues (e.g., drafty windows or areas with direct sunlight).
24-28 2 Marginal Slightly warm for some employees. 1. Ensure ventilation and cooling systems are operational.
2. Offer localized fans or cooling solutions for those who feel warm.
28-32 3 High Comfort decreases, potential decrease in productivity. 1. Increase air circulation and consider bringing in additional cooling units.
2. Recommend lighter clothing and ensure hydration stations are available.
3. Consider temporary breaks to cooler areas if needed.
>32 4 Very High Heat stress possible, significant discomfort, and reduced productivity. 1. Implement heat stress protocols including regular breaks in cooler areas.
2. Consider letting employees work remotely or re-scheduling work hours to cooler times of the day.
3. Seek expert advice for long-term cooling solutions for the facility.
Relative Humidity Level (%) Comfort Index Value Level of Concern Symptoms/Effects (8 hours Exposure) Action Recommendation
<30 3 High Dry air can lead to skin irritation, respiratory symptoms, and increased susceptibility to colds and respiratory infections. Electronic equipment may be at risk due to static electricity. 1. Use humidifiers to increase humidity levels.
2. Regularly check HVAC settings to ensure they're not excessively removing moisture.
3. Encourage employees to stay hydrated.
4. Use anti-static sprays or other methods to manage static electricity.
30-50 0 Low Humidity in this range is generally considered comfortable and poses minimal risk to health or equipment. 1. Monitor and maintain this humidity range for optimal comfort.
2. Regular HVAC maintenance to ensure consistent humidity levels.
50-70 1 Acceptable Slightly elevated levels can lead to feelings of stuffiness and can encourage mold growth. 1. Consider using dehumidifiers in areas where humidity tends to be higher.
2. Increase ventilation in areas prone to moisture accumulation.
3. Check for any water leaks or sources of excess moisture and address them.
70-90 2 Marginal High risk of mold growth. Possible respiratory and skin irritation. Electronic equipment may malfunction. 1. Immediate use of dehumidifiers is essential.
2. Inspect for water leaks, poor ventilation, or other issues leading to excess moisture.
3. Encourage employees to report any areas of mold or water accumulation.
4. Seek a professional assessment if mold is found.
>90 4 Very High Extremely uncomfortable. Serious risk of mold growth, structural damage, and equipment malfunction. 1. Immediate action is required: deploy industrial dehumidifiers.
2. Limit occupancy of areas with extremely high humidity until resolved.
3. Engage professionals to identify the root cause of the high humidity and rectify it.
4. Inspect and repair any structural issues or leaks contributing to the problem.
Illumininance Level (Lux) Comfort Index Value Level of Concern Symptoms/Effects (8 hours Exposure) Action Recommendation
<100 3 High Too dim for most tasks, which can strain the eyes and lead to errors. 1. Increase lighting in the area, either by using brighter bulbs or adding more light fixtures.
2. Consider relocating employees to well-lit areas for tasks requiring precision.
3. Use task lighting, like desk lamps, to provide focused light where needed.
100-250 0 Low Adequate for circulation areas and rooms where minimal visual tasks are performed. 1. Monitor to ensure this range is consistent.
2. Consider task lighting or spot lighting for any intricate tasks.
250-500 1 Acceptable Ideal for general office work, computer tasks, and reading. 1. Maintain lighting fixtures and regularly replace aging or dimming bulbs.
2. Ensure even distribution of light to avoid shadows or overly lit areas.
500-1000 2 Marginal Might be excessively bright for typical office tasks but suitable for detailed inspections. 1. Use dimmers or adjustable lighting fixtures to modulate brightness based on the task at hand.
2. Ensure that lights don't create glare on screens or reflective surfaces.
3. Adjustable shades or diffusers can be used to optimize lighting conditions.
>1000 4 Very High Excessively bright. Can cause glare, discomfort, and visual fatigue. Not typical for regular office settings but can be used in scenarios requiring intense illumination, like surgery or detailed inspections. 1. Limit exposure durations to very high lux levels.
2. Provide protective eyewear if the high lux level is required for specific tasks.
3. Use shades, blinds, or diffusers to control natural light influx in areas where sunlight leads to excessive brightness.

Noise Level (dBA) Noise Index Value Level of Concern Symptoms/Effects (8 hours Exposure) Action Recommendation
<45 0 Low Satisfactory noise level with minimal impact on concentration and well-being. 1. Maintain a quiet and comfortable work environment.
2. Encourage the use of headphones or quiet spaces for focused work.
3. Ensure office equipment and HVAC systems operate at low noise levels.
45 - 55 1 Acceptable Moderate noise levels that may be noticeable but generally do not cause disruption. 1. Consider implementing sound-absorbing materials or partitions.
2. Provide acoustic panels or ceiling tiles to reduce reverberation.
3. Encourage the use of designated quiet zones for tasks requiring concentration.
55 - 65 2 Marginal Increased noise levels that may lead to decreased productivity and increased stress. 1. Conduct a noise assessment to identify and address specific sources of high noise.
2. Introduce noise-canceling technologies or white noise machines.
3. Encourage the use of noise-canceling headphones for employees who need concentration.
65 - 75 3 High High noise levels that can lead to discomfort, reduced productivity, and potential health issues. 1. Implement measures to reduce noise from equipment and machinery.
2. Consider rearranging workspaces to minimize noise exposure.
3. Provide quiet zones or soundproof rooms for tasks requiring intense concentration.
>75 4 Very High Very high noise levels that pose a risk of hearing damage and significant health issues. 1. Implement engineering controls to reduce noise at the source.
2. Provide hearing protection, such as earplugs or earmuffs, for employees in noisy areas.
3. Conduct regular noise monitoring and employee training on hearing protection.
HCHO Level (ppb) HCHO Index Value Level of Concern Symptoms/Effects (8 hours Exposure) Action Recommendation
<20 0 Low Satisfactory air quality with minimal risk. 1. Maintain regular air quality monitoring.
2. Implement general air quality management practices.
3. Use formaldehyde-free building materials and products.
20 - 50 1 Acceptable Low concerns, particularly for sensitive individuals. 1. Continue regular air quality monitoring.
2. Identify and address specific sources of formaldehyde, such as furniture or building materials.
3. Enhance ventilation in affected areas.
50 - 100 2 Marginal Unhealthy for sensitive groups; general public not likely to be affected. 1. Increase air quality monitoring frequency.
2. Implement source control measures, such as using low-emission products and materials.
3. Improve ventilation systems in buildings, especially in areas with higher formaldehyde concentrations.
100 - 150 3 High Unhealthy; general public may begin to experience health effects. 1. Implement immediate actions to reduce formaldehyde concentrations, including identifying and replacing sources of contamination.
2. Enhance ventilation systems and consider the use of air purifiers with formaldehyde filters.
3. Communicate health advisories and encourage sensitive individuals to take precautions.
>150 4 Very High Health alert; everyone may begin to experience more serious health effects. 1. Implement emergency measures to reduce formaldehyde exposure, such as evacuating affected areas.
2. Engage with local health authorities and consider temporary shutdowns or restrictions until indoor air quality is improved.
3. Investigate and address sources of elevated formaldehyde levels and implement long-term indoor air quality management strategies.
